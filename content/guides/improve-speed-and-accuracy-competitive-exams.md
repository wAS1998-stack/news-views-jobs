In competitive government exams, knowing the subject is not enough. With dozens of questions to attempt in a tight time limit and negative marking punishing wrong answers, two skills decide your score: speed and accuracy. Many well-prepared candidates underperform simply because they are too slow, too careless, or both. The good news is that speed and accuracy are trainable skills, not fixed talents. This guide explains exactly how to build them.

## Why both speed and accuracy matter

Speed without accuracy is dangerous, because in exams with negative marking, fast but careless attempts lose marks. Accuracy without speed is equally limiting, because a candidate who solves perfectly but slowly cannot attempt enough questions to score well. The goal is the balance of the two: attempting as many questions as possible while keeping your accuracy high enough that negative marking does not erode your score. Understanding that you need both, in balance, is the first step to improving them.

## Build accuracy first, then speed

A common mistake is chasing speed from the start. This usually backfires, producing fast but error-ridden attempts. The better sequence is to build accuracy first. When you are learning a topic, focus on solving questions correctly, even if slowly, until the method is solid and your error rate is low. Speed then comes naturally through repetition — as a method becomes familiar, you perform it faster without sacrificing accuracy. Trying to be fast before you are accurate simply ingrains mistakes. Get it right first; getting it fast follows.

## Master your fundamentals and tables

Much of the speed in quantitative sections comes from strong fundamentals. Knowing your multiplication tables, squares, cubes, common fractions and percentage equivalents by heart removes the small delays that add up across a paper. Similarly, in reasoning, recognising question types instantly saves precious seconds. Time invested in making your fundamentals automatic pays back many times over in the exam, because your mind spends its energy on solving rather than on basic calculation.

## Learn and practise shortcuts

Every competitive subject has shortcuts and techniques that solve questions faster than the textbook method. Learning these — and, crucially, practising them until they are second nature — is a major source of speed. But a shortcut you have not practised enough is unreliable under pressure, so do not try to use a half-learned trick in the actual exam. Build your shortcuts during practice, test them in mocks, and rely only on those you can execute confidently and accurately.

## Smart question selection: know when to skip

Perhaps the most underrated exam skill is choosing which questions to attempt and which to leave. Not every question deserves your time. The skill is to quickly identify the questions you can solve confidently, secure those marks, and skip or postpone the ones that are time-consuming or uncertain. Stubbornly fighting a single hard question can cost you several easier marks elsewhere. Training yourself to make these decisions quickly, during mocks, is one of the fastest ways to improve your real score.

## Use sectional time management

Going into an exam without a time plan is a recipe for running out of time. Decide in advance roughly how long to spend on each section, based on your strengths and the marks available, and stick to it. Set internal checkpoints so a single section cannot quietly consume time you needed elsewhere. A simple, rehearsed time plan — practised in mocks until it feels natural — keeps you in control and ensures you reach every section rather than leaving easy marks unattempted at the end.

## Practise under timed conditions

Speed and accuracy improve only through practice that mimics the real exam. Solving questions leisurely builds knowledge but not exam skills. Regularly practise sets and full-length mocks under strict time limits, so your mind learns to perform under pressure. Timed practice trains your pace, sharpens your question-selection instinct, and builds the stamina to stay accurate through a long paper. The exam hall should feel familiar because you have rehearsed its conditions many times.

## Analyse mocks to fix the real problems

Taking mocks is only half the value; analysing them is where speed and accuracy actually improve. After every mock, study your errors honestly. Separate them into types: concept gaps, careless mistakes, and time-pressure errors. Each type needs a different fix — concept gaps need revision, careless mistakes need more careful execution, and time-pressure errors need better question selection or pacing. Targeting the real causes of your errors, rather than just attempting more questions, is what produces steady, measurable improvement.

## Stay calm to stay accurate

Accuracy collapses under panic. When you rush in anxiety, you misread questions and make careless errors. Building exam temperament — through repeated timed practice and a clear strategy — keeps you calm enough to read carefully and think clearly. A composed candidate attempting fewer questions accurately often outscores a panicked one attempting many carelessly. Treat calmness not as a personality trait but as a skill you build through preparation, because it directly protects your accuracy.

## Common mistakes to avoid

The errors that hold candidates back are predictable: chasing speed before accuracy, relying on half-learned shortcuts, stubbornly fighting hard questions, entering the exam without a time plan, practising without time limits, and taking mocks without analysing them. Avoiding these, and addressing the real causes of your errors, transforms both your speed and your accuracy over a few months of focused practice.

## A weekly practice routine to build both

Putting the principles together, a simple weekly routine builds speed and accuracy steadily. Early in the week, focus on accuracy: solve topic-wise questions carefully, prioritising correctness over pace, and review the methods behind any you get wrong. Midweek, introduce timed sectional practice, attempting sets within a fixed time to start building speed on top of your now-solid accuracy. Towards the weekend, take a full-length mock under strict exam conditions, then spend dedicated time analysing it — categorising your errors into concept gaps, careless mistakes and time-pressure errors, and planning fixes for each. Keep a log of recurring weak areas and revisit them. This rhythm — accuracy-focused practice, then timed practice, then a mock and its analysis — ensures you are always building both skills in the right order. Repeated over several weeks, it produces exactly the combination the exam rewards: attempting more questions, more accurately, in less time.

## Frequently asked questions

**Should I focus on speed or accuracy first?**
Build accuracy first. Solve correctly until your method is solid, and speed will follow naturally through repetition. Chasing speed first usually ingrains mistakes.

**How do I increase my calculation speed?**
Master your tables, squares, fractions and percentage equivalents, and practise shortcuts until they are automatic. Strong fundamentals remove the small delays that slow you down.

**How do I decide which questions to skip?**
Quickly secure the questions you can solve confidently, and skip or postpone time-consuming or uncertain ones. Practise this decision in mocks until it becomes instinctive.

**Why do I make silly mistakes even when I know the topic?**
Usually due to rushing or anxiety. Build accuracy through careful timed practice and stay calm, since panic is the main cause of careless errors.

**How important are mock tests for speed and accuracy?**
Essential. Timed mocks train your pace and question selection, and analysing them reveals the real causes of your errors so you can fix them.

**Can speed and accuracy really be improved, or are they fixed?**
They are trainable skills, not fixed talents. With the right sequence — accuracy first, then speed through practice — most candidates improve significantly over a few months.

**How long before I see improvement in speed?**
With consistent timed practice and mock analysis, most candidates notice steady improvement within a few weeks, as accuracy solidifies and methods become faster through repetition.

**Is it better to attempt more questions or fewer accurate ones?**
Aim for the balance: attempt as many as you can while keeping accuracy high enough that negative marking does not erode your score. Accurate attempts always beat reckless ones.

**Does practising more questions automatically improve speed?**
Only if you practise correctly. Build accuracy first, then practise under timed conditions and analyse your errors. Mindless volume without analysis improves little.

**How do mock tests help with speed and accuracy?**
Mocks train you to perform at exam pace and, through honest analysis, reveal the real causes of your errors so you can fix them and improve both skills steadily.

## A final word

Speed and accuracy are not gifts some candidates are born with — they are skills you build deliberately. Start by getting your methods right, make your fundamentals automatic, learn and practise reliable shortcuts, and train your question-selection and time management through timed mocks. Above all, analyse your mistakes and stay calm. Do this consistently, and you will walk into the exam able to attempt more questions correctly in less time, which is exactly what a competitive exam rewards.
